Economic development in the future.

Changing the perception of the Economic development in the future

What exactly is economic development and how has the concept evolved over the years? While the economic aspect may seem relatively simple, a steady rise in per capita income is only one part of the equation. It is not enough to guarantee development or economic growth on its own.
The distribution of this rising income among the population is also an essential aspect of economic development. Income inequality and poverty are two crucial factors that must be considered. If the average income increases, but the inequality of its distribution also increases, then economic development could be seen as a negative outcome from an egalitarian perspective. Likewise, if poverty increases and more people fall below socially acceptable levels of income, it is another negative mark to be set against rising average income when assessing economic development. Whether poverty actually decreases or not will depend on the interaction between average income and inequality, and which of the two forces dominates empirically.
Focusing solely on measured market income misses out on valuable resources that are not appropriately priced in the market, such as the environment. Rising national income as conventionally measured does not account for the loss of irreplaceable environmental resources at the national level or the irreversible impact on the planet due to climate change.
In conclusion, economic development is not only about rising income levels, but also about how income is distributed and the impact of development on the environment. Therefore, it is necessary to broaden our perspective and consider a more comprehensive approach to measure economic development in the future.
