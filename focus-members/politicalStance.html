Changing the perception of the Political stance in the future

It has been a long-standing opinion in certain conservative circles in the West that political parties are in decline, viewed as a divisive force among citizens and a threat to national unity. In some European countries, such as France, right-wing political organizations have even refused to call themselves parties, instead using terms such as movement, union, federation, and centre. While it is true that the major contemporary European and American parties appear old and rigid in comparison with their condition at the turn of the century or immediately following World War I, it cannot be denied that political parties are growing in size and number.
At the turn of the 20th century, political parties were confined mainly to Europe and North America, and they were quite weak or nonexistent elsewhere. In the early 21st century, parties could be found practically everywhere in the world. In Europe and North America, there were generally far more people holding membership in parties than prior to 1914. Parties of the early 21st century were larger, stronger, and better-organized than those of the late 19th century. While parties in industrialized countries, especially in western Europe, have become less revolutionary and innovative, this factor may explain the rigid and worn-out image that they sometimes present. But even this phenomenon is found only in a limited area and may, perhaps, pass.
In the future, it is possible that political parties will continue to evolve and adapt to changing circumstances. They will need to address the concerns of a diverse and increasingly global population, while balancing the need for stability and continuity with the demand for innovation and progress. The role of political parties in shaping public policy and representing the interests of their constituents will remain crucial, even as the political landscape shifts and new challenges arise.
